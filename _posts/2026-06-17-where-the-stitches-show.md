In the cold months in rural Japan, four hundred years ago, farming families <span class="m-underline m-lazuli">could not afford to throw cloth away</span>. Cotton was scarce in the north; what they had they kept, and what wore through they reinforced. So in winter, when the fields were quiet, they sewed. Indigo-dyed work clothes, patched with a running stitch in undyed white thread — rows of it, close and even, building up at the elbows and knees and shoulders where the body wears the cloth thin. They called it **sashiko**: *little stabs*. Small, patient, repeated.

{% include definition.html term="Sashiko" body="A Japanese reinforcement-and-mending technique using simple running stitches, traditionally white cotton thread on indigo cloth. Practiced communally and seasonally, mostly by farming women, from roughly the 1600s. The visible stitching strengthens worn fabric — but it also leaves a legible record of every place the garment was kept rather than discarded." %}

Here is the part that matters for us. <span class="m-underline m-coral">They did not hide the stitches.</span> They *could* have. A more anxious culture, a more disposable one, would have buried the repair, made the mend invisible, pretended the cloth had never torn. Sashiko does the opposite. The stitching is on the outside, in a contrasting color, plain to anyone who looks. The mend is the most visible thing on the garment.
{% include m-note.html text="← read this one out loud. it's the hinge of the whole essay." color="coral" side="left" %}
{% include m-mark.html type="exclaim" color="coral" side="right" %}

Why? Because the visible part isn't decoration. It's evidence. Each constellation of stitches says: *this cloth was worth keeping. Someone sat with it in winter. This garment has been carried inside the practice of a life.* The wear is not shameful. <span class="m-hl m-happy">The wear is the proof that the thing was used, loved, lived in</span> — and the repair is the proof that it was kept.

This is the lineage Drex descends from, and I mean *descends* literally, not as a marketing flourish. The Visible Menders trace back through repair circles and quilting bees and household economies of mending that have existed, in some form, in nearly every culture that ever had cloth and could not waste it. The practice is older than any of us and <span class="m-underline m-coral">it was never really about the cloth</span>. It was about the gathering — winter, the quiet table, hands busy, the company of others doing the same patient work.
{% include m-note.html text="*descends*, literally — not a flourish. we mean it." color="grass" side="left" %}

There's a phrase that comes out of the same world: *wabi-sabi* — the beauty of the worn, the incomplete, the impermanent. But I find sashiko more useful than the philosophy, because sashiko is <span class="m-underline m-lazuli">a thing you *do*</span>, not a thing you contemplate. It is a craft held across four centuries by people who never wrote a manifesto about it. They just kept showing up in winter, kept stabbing the little stitches in, kept the cloth alive, kept each other company.
{% include m-note.html text="a thing you DO, not a thing you contemplate. say this out loud." color="coral" side="left" %}
{% include m-mark.html type="asterisk" color="coral" side="right" %}

That's the whole inheritance. <span class="m-hl m-happy">Keep the thing. Keep the company. Let the stitches show.</span>
