Every few weeks someone asks me, kindly, what the app *does*. They want a feature list. I understand the instinct — it's how we've been taught to ask after software — but the question has the order backwards, and the order is the whole thing.

Drex is not an app that has a community attached. Drex is a community of practice that needs, among other things, some software. The room comes first. The room is the product. Everything we build is in service of the room continuing — between sessions, across cities, across the months a person spends moving from the edge of a practice toward its center.

{% include callout.html kind="for-drex" title="For Drex" body="If a feature does not help a specific person make something with other people, or help that making be seen, or help the next person find their way in — it is not a Drex feature. It is a distraction wearing a Drex sticker." %}

Here is what I mean by a room. A small number of people — five to twelve — in a space, making the thing they came to make, in the presence of others doing the same. A mending circle. A drawing table. A thrift haul laid out and reckoned with. Nobody is performing. Nobody is optimizing. Someone who has done this for years sits next to someone doing it for the first time, and the newcomer learns not by being lectured but by being *near* — by watching how a veteran holds the cloth, by being handed the good scissors, by being asked, gently, to bring the thing they were afraid to bring.

That is not a metaphor for what software can simulate. That is the irreducible unit. The technology's only honest job is to make more of those rooms possible and to carry what happens inside them to the people who weren't there yet.

{% include pullquote.html text="A community of practice is not an audience you've gathered. It is a craft you are keeping alive together, and you happen to be doing it in the same place." %}

This is why our metric is not subscribers and not downloads and not the number every other company in our category reports proudly to its board. Our metric is **bridge crossings** — a stranger becoming a reader, a reader walking into their first room, a first-timer coming back, a returner bringing a friend. Each crossing is a person moving closer to the center of the practice. A hundred thousand views that produce no crossing is a failure dressed as a success. Twelve people who would come to every future session is the business.

The software will get good. We'll ship the things you'd expect us to ship. But I want to be precise about the order, because the order is what keeps a company honest when the growth-chasing instincts arrive — and they always arrive. We resist them by remembering that we did not set out to build software. We set out to keep a practice alive, the way every craft worth practicing has been kept alive: in small rooms, among apprentices, by people who showed up and made something real.

The room is the product. The app is how the room keeps going when you've gone home.
