**To those in AFRICAAM 263,**

I'm grateful for the time we spent together, and thank you to Professor Sam for the opportunity to speak.

Our time together has proven to be a seminal moment in helping drex contextualize our mission to "cultivate people's gifts" into today's world. The mission of drex comes from the eternal Good News of **Stewardship** — a Christian principle which makes known that <span class="m-hl m-happy">we've all been given unrepeatable and important gifts and talents</span>, and that it is our duty to steward those gifts into the world for the goodness of others. This Good News is perennial. Regardless of the technological innovations, we as humans have continued to be blessed with — and responsible for — the actualization of Stewardship in the world. <span class="m-underline m-grass">Even now, in the current technological age of AI.</span>
{% include m-note.html text="the whole letter turns on this one word: stewardship." color="grass" side="right" %}

{% include definition.html term="Stewardship" body="A Christian principle: each of us is given unrepeatable gifts and talents, and is responsible for bringing them into the world for the good of others. The conviction is *perennial* — it does not expire when the technology changes." %}

Tracing the history of how humans have become *even more essential* in the process of labor as technology has evolved lets us see something concretely: when the means of production become cheap, human focus moves <span class="m-underline m-coral">**upstream**</span> — to specifying intent for, and verifying the output of, what is being produced. This concept, first brought to my attention by my friend and co-founder Sue, quickly became intuitive in the very field of manufacturing — one I'm deeply familiar with from my studies at Stanford and my work at Apple and Tesla.
{% include m-note.html text="upstream — the whole idea, in one word →" color="coral" side="right" %}

The story begins with **John Hall**, who recognized that more people could make parts well if they focused on the <span class="m-underline m-lazuli">fixturing</span> — so that making a good cut became more intuitive and accessible. What was once expensive to produce, by nature of fewer people being able to make it well, fell in expense. And so the skill that became most valuable was <span class="m-hl m-happy">specifying what kind of fixture ought to be used, and what the outcome should look like</span> once machining is done.
{% include m-note.html text="the skill that survives isn't the cutting — it's saying what \"good\" is." color="lazuli" side="left" %}

This lineage of human specification and verification has now evolved into two quintessential practices — **GD&T** (Geometric Dimensioning & Tolerancing) and **SPC** (Statistical Process Control) — without which modern manufacturing and assembly would be impossible, and goods would be far less accessible. <span class="m-underline m-grass">You hold the proof in your pocket:</span>

{% include definition.html term="Specification · GD&T" body="**It always closes flush.** Open any iPhone teardown: hundreds of parts, and the phone never bulges. GD&T is the contract for how each part must meet the next — so the designer never worries about *how* a part gets made." %}

{% include definition.html term="Verification · SPC" body="**Millions match the intent.** Across the millions sold, the iPhone in each hand matches what the designer intended. SPC is the contract that holds at scale — again freeing the designer from the *how*." %}

In a perfect world, a designer would only communicate intent (specification) and recognize the result (verification), and never worry about how a part gets made (execution). But what we learn from actually producing parts is that it takes many iterative runs to fine-tune both the specification *and* the system used to get the output — the **harness**. In manufacturing, the harness is the actual factory; the goal is for parts to be made seamlessly, without constant intervention. When a run fails to meet the spec, the engineers whose job is to refine the harness take progressively deeper looks under the hood, so the next run produces what was intended.
{% include m-note.html text="the harness = the system you tend, not the thing you make." color="lazuli" side="left" %}

When making parts got cheap, mechanical engineers didn't disappear — they moved their talents upstream, to three disciplines:

- <span class="m-underline m-grass">**Specification**</span> — *say what's worth making.* In manufacturing, **GD&T**: how parts must meet. With AI: **name your intent.**
- <span class="m-underline m-lazuli">**Verification**</span> — *know it meets your intent.* In manufacturing, **SPC**: proof they match, at scale. With AI: **confirm it matches.**
- <span class="m-underline m-coral">**Harness**</span> — *tend the system that makes it.* In manufacturing, the factory, refined each run. With AI: **the tools and context you give it.**
{% include m-mark.html type="caret" color="grass" side="left" %}

{% include pullquote.html text="Humans have not become irrelevant — we've become more essential, upstream." %}

In the age of AI, the good news of the human's role is the same. We haven't become irrelevant — we've moved upstream, to the more essential gift we possess: conceptualizing a vision for how to bring our gifts into the world, whether large or small. And we can now draw on the same three disciplines, along with AI — which has made production cheap for almost any action that requires text (letters on a keyboard) — to execute and steward our gifts into the world.
{% include m-note.html text="same move — new machine." color="coral" side="right" %}

{% include callout.html kind="note" title="One thing I'd ask of you" body="Take something you'd love to make — an essay, a project, a question you can't stop circling — and try the three disciplines on it. **Name what you intend** (specification), **name how you'll know the output meets your intent** (verification), and **tend the system that gets you there** (the harness) — with AI doing the execution. Then write me back and tell me what you made, or where you got stuck." %}

In our talk on April 29th, this is where I feel I left you — without a framework for *how* to cultivate your gifts using AI, after having convinced you that your gifts had moved upstream to specifying intent and verifying output. I was very touched by the actionable questions you asked about applying this to your coursework — one of you thinking through what it means for writing essays; another asking what this frame would mean for someone in middle school, still learning what, for example, a "good" essay even looks like. I hope this letter brings it around full circle, with conceptual grounding, and encourages you to be <span class="m-hl m-happy">a good steward of all you've been given</span>.

With gratitude,

Chielo
